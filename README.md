## **SOURCES ON BOT ACCOUNTS & AUTOMATION**

   A research analysis of ~94,500 YouTube comments detected 599 bot accounts (‚â§1% of accounts in the sample) that generated ~11.6% of all comments and impacted nearly 39% of videos analyzed. ([Factcheck][1])

   Separate analysis confirms coordinated botnet behavior affecting comments across hundreds of videos, indicating bots can be concentrated and impactful even if they represent a small fraction of accounts. ([Factcheck][2])

3. **General difficulty in bot detection and varying prevalence estimates**
   Academic research notes that bot definitions vary widely and that automated account prevalence is difficult to measure precisely, with different platforms and tools producing disparate estimates. ([SpringerLink][3])

4. **Bot prevalence estimates on other social platforms (e.g., X/Twitter)**
   Independent analyses of X (formerly Twitter) account samples have suggested that a substantial share of accounts‚Äîpotentially around 64% in a 1.269 M-account sample‚Äîexhibit bot-like characteristics, implying high bot prevalence in aggressive detection models. ([X (formerly Twitter)][4])

   In some social media contexts (e.g., Twitter datasets), nearly half of accounts were classified as bots using combined detection methods in scholarly work ‚Äî illustrating how classification thresholds influence bot prevalence figures. ([Nature][5])

6. **Bot prevalence perception vs. official estimates**
   Research on perceptions versus reported figures shows that people commonly estimate social bot prevalence (e.g., ~32‚Äì38%) well above many platform reports, highlighting public concern around bot numbers. ([Nature][6])

7. **Historical bot prevalence estimates on Twitter**
   Earlier research (2017) estimated that 9‚Äì15% of active Twitter accounts were bots using machine learning detection frameworks, demonstrating that bot prevalence is not a new concern. ([arXiv][7])

8. **Studies on platform content corrections related to artificial engagement**
   Research analyzing fake view removal on YouTube shows that platforms actively correct for artificial engagement (views, likes, etc.) ‚Äî indicating that bots are recognized and filtered, but also that their activity can be widespread before removal. ([Nature][8])

9. **Industry summaries highlighting uncertainty in bot counting**
   Industry summaries and bot prevalence charts note that companies often report lower bot counts than some external or independent research models find, due to different definitions and detection scopes. ([Reporterzy][9])

    Surveys like those by Pew Research Center show strong public awareness and concern about bot accounts on social media, which reinforces that bot prevalence is both a research and public perception issue. ([Pew Research Center][10])
